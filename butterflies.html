MONARCH BUTTERFLY

Danaus plexippus

Kakahu or Kahuku

This is probably the best-known butterfly in New Zealand, originally from North America and first recorded here in the mid-1800s. It is considered a native because it flew/blew to NZ.


Biostatus

Native (flew/blew from North America)

Host Plant

Milkweed, e.g. swan plant (Gomphocarpus fruticosus), giant swan plant (G. physocarpus), and tropical milkweed (Asclepias curassavica)

Egg

Creamy and ridged, usually on underside of new growth.Takes about five days to hatch.

Larva

Yellow/black/white stripes, five instars. Average of 14 days before pupation.

Pupa

Yellow/black/white stripes, five instars. Average of 14 days before pupation.

Adult/Imago

Orange, black and white. Male has two scent glands on hindwings, and thinner veins than female.

Wingspan

100 mm.

Overwinters

As adults in conifer trees.

Range

Naturally as far south as 45 degrees.

SOUTHERN BLUE BUTTERFLY

Zizina oxleyi

Pepe ao uri

The Southern Blue is a pretty little New Zealand species, now missing from most of New Zealand apart from the south-east of the South Island.

While currently listed as ‘Not Threatened’, the southern blue is in fact under threat from an Australian invading blue butterfly (Z. labradus).


Biostatus

Endemic

Host Plant

Low-growing native brooms (Carmichaelia spp) and low-growing Fabaceae (e.g. clover, trefoil).

Egg

Laid singly, pale green turning blue-green. 4-6 days.

Larva

Up to 14 mm, 5 weeks.

Adult/Imago

Females dull grey more than blue. Both genders have silvery grey undersides with dark zigzag bands and a banded border.

Wingspan

17-30 mm.

Overwinters

Unknown

Range

Eastern side of South Island.

RED ADMIRAL BUTTERFLY

Vanessa gonerilla gonerilla

Kahukura

NZ's beautiful red admiral, once common. Adults overwinter and can be seen in areas where their host plant is still present - but sadly their host plant has been seen as a weed and removed from urban areas.

There are other red admirals around the world (viz V. atalanta in North America/Europe and V. indica in Asia) but this one is considered the most beautiful with its colours truly symbolic of NZ: the red of the pohutukawa and the blue, the shades of paua.


Biostatus

Endemic

Host Plant

In the wild Urtica ferox (Ongaonga), but it will also feed on U. incisa and U. urens. Also know to feed on Parietaria debilis, P. australis, P. cardiostegia, P. judaica.

Egg

Green, ribbed and barrel-shaped, generally laid singly on leaves of nettles.

Larva

Five instars. Difficult to distinguish from a yellow admiral larva.

Pupa

Pupa about 20 mm.

Adult/Imago

Top of forewings mostly black with a central bright red bar running from front edge. White spots, fringed with light blue near tips of forewings. Hindwings are dark reddish brown, with a red patch containing four black circles. Centre of each circle is pale blue. Underside of hindwings cryptic. Underside of forewings has blue eyespot bracketed by red and white arcs.

Wingspan

50-60 mm wingspan.>

Overwinters

Overwinter as adults. Long-lived, surviving up to six months (summer) and nine months (overwintering).

Range

Most frequently seen during summer.
